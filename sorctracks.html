SORCTracks: A Visualization of Temporary Health Service Disruptions Across Alberta

SORC is primarily led by students in the University of Alberta MD Program and has a strong interest in Alberta's ongoing health service access challenges. This interest has led SORC to develop projects that make fragmented public health system data more accessible, interpretable, and useful for communities, clinicians, researchers, and policymakers.

SORCTracks is an independent public-data visualization tool that reconstructs publicly archived Alberta Health Services temporary service disruption postings into an interactive, searchable map. Users can explore where, when, and how often temporary service disruptions have been publicly posted across Alberta.

The tool currently includes a completed Emergency Department layer and a searchable reference layer of Alberta Health Services locations. Additional service layers, including maternity and obstetric services, acute care and inpatient services, surgery and operating rooms, endoscopy, urgent and ambulatory care, orthopedics, long-term care, home care, rehabilitation, detox, ophthalmology, and cardiology, will be added as those data are analyzed.

SORCTracks is not a real-time operational dashboard. It is a reconstructed research and public-information resource based on archived public postings. Data will be updated quarterly as additional archived notices are reviewed and incorporated.

Interpretive Cautions
  • This is a public-record reconstruction, not an operational gold standard. Some disruptions may not have been publicly posted, archived consistently, or timed defensibly from available source material.
  • SORCTracks is not real-time. It reflects archived public postings through the data-through date listed above. It is not an Alberta Health Services product and is not affiliated with AHS.
  • Estimated hours reflect publicly reconstructable disruption intervals. Unioned hours represent unique site-level disruption time after overlapping intervals are merged, and should not be treated as a precise operational measure.
  • This tool supports research and public understanding. It is not intended for clinical or operational decision-making.

Temporary Emergency Department Service Disruptions in Alberta: A Retrospective Archive-Based Analysis of Burden, Distribution, and Stated Causes, 2021–2026
Tung A, Nguyen V et al.
Abstract
Background
Temporary emergency department (ED) service disruptions in Alberta are publicly posted by Alberta Health Services (AHS), but their cumulative burden has not been systematically reconstructed. We aimed to quantify ED disruption burden in Alberta using archived public records.
Methods
We conducted a retrospective archive-based descriptive study of AHS temporary service disruption postings from August 1, 2021 to March 31, 2026. ED-related records were identified from two public archive streams: dated snapshot pages of active service disruptions and the AHS news/notice archive. Source records were treated as events before within-source deduplication and as episodes after deduplication of repeated postings referring to the same underlying disruption. Closure intervals were reconstructed from public wording, clipped to the study window, and merged within site-year to estimate unioned ED disruption hours. Notice-derived episodes underwent cross-source reconciliation against the snapshot-derived corpus, with targeted manual review and fixed-interval recovery when supported by the source text.
Results
The archive-sourced analytic dataset contained 1,023 ED episodes after cross-source reconciliation, including 936 snapshot-archive episodes and 87 notice-archive episodes. Of these, 964 were accounted for with existing captured hours, 44 required manual recovery, and 15 were excluded for known reasons. Final estimated burden was 147,456.5 unioned ED disruption hours. Annual burden was 6,289.0 h in the partial 2021 period, 27,951.5 h in 2022, 38,504.3 h in 2023, 33,433.2 h in 2024, 32,547.5 h in 2025, and 8,731.0 h in the partial 2026 period. Across all years, the highest-burden sites were Consort, Hardisty, Boyle, Grimshaw, and Two Hills.
Conclusion
Publicly documented ED service disruptions in Alberta represent a substantial and recurrent loss of emergency care access, with burden concentrated in a limited number of communities. From a public health and health-system perspective, these findings support routine province-wide surveillance of ED disruptions, more transparent reporting of service interruptions, and targeted workforce stabilization efforts in communities experiencing repeated closure burden.

Rural Maternity Service Instability in Alberta: An Archive-Based Analysis of Publicly Posted Hospital Service Disruptions
Tung A, Nguyen V et al.
Abstract
Background
Access to local maternity care in rural and regional communities may be disrupted by temporary loss of obstetrical services, cesarean section capability, anesthesia coverage, or delivery support. In Alberta, many of these disruptions are publicly posted by Alberta Health Services, but their frequency, geographic distribution, and patterns have not been systematically described. We aimed to characterize publicly posted maternity service capability disruptions in Alberta using archived public records.
Methods
We performed a retrospective descriptive study of archived Alberta Health Services temporary service disruption postings. Records were drawn from dated snapshot pages of active disruptions and the Alberta Health Services notice archive. Eligible records included postings explicitly affecting local maternity capability, including obstetrical service disruptions, loss of cesarean section or epidural capability, and mixed-service disruptions involving obstetrics when delivery care or maternity backup was explicitly affected. Records were standardized by site and categorized by disruption type. Analyses were descriptive and focused on temporal, geographic, and service-pattern characteristics of publicly posted maternity-related disruptions.

Collaborators would review findings for clinical plausibility, help contextualize disruption patterns relative to rural obstetrical access challenges, advise on service-layer classification, and contribute to manuscript framing. Authorship would be offered for contributions meeting ICMJE criteria. To express interest, contact us at info@sorc.ca.
