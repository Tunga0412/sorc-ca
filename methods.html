SORCTracks

Methods

SORCTracks is a public-record reconstruction. For interpretive caveats that apply before consulting these methods, see the SORCTracks page.

This page is a web-facing summary of the methods underlying SORCTracks. The full methodology documentation, data dictionary, and audit files are available through the OSF project: SORCTracks parent project. Emergency Department layer documentation is available in the ED component. The methods documented here apply to the Emergency Department layer and reflect the procedures in use as of Beta v0.2 (May 12, 2026). Methods for subsequent service layers will be documented as those layers are released.

Data Sources

Data are drawn from two public Alberta Health Services (AHS) sources: archived snapshots of active temporary service disruptions and the AHS notice/news archive.

The snapshot archive provides recurring records of disruptions active on specific dates. The notice archive is used to identify additional ED-related disruptions that may be missed or underrepresented in the snapshot stream.

Definitions
Raw record / event
A source-specific archive entry before deduplication.
Episode
A deduplicated ED disruption, produced after repeated postings referring to the same underlying disruption are collapsed into a single record.
Closure interval
The reconstructed start-to-end period during which ED service was unavailable or reduced, according to the public wording of the source record.
Unioned hours
Unique site-level disruption hours after overlapping intervals are merged within each site and year. This measure avoids double-counting when multiple disruptions overlap at the same site.
Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria
Included
  • Records referring to temporary ED closures or ED service disruptions during the study window.
Excluded
  • Non-ED service disruptions.
  • Partial capacity or bed reductions without ED closure.
  • Staffing issues described without sufficient timing to support a defensible interval.
  • Events outside the study window.
  • Reopen-only notices without a defensible start time.
Interval Reconstruction

Intervals were reconstructed from public wording using rule-based parsing. Explicit start and end times were retained directly from the source text.

Recurring overnight, weekend, or reduced-hours schedules were converted into intervals when the wording supported a defensible interpretation. Ambiguous or incomplete wording was not imputed unless a fixed interval could be manually justified from the source material.

Duplicate Handling

Repeated records from snapshot pages were deduplicated into episodes using site, timing, service, and text fields. Notice-derived episodes were reconciled against the snapshot-derived corpus to identify duplicates, overlaps, and distinct events not captured in the snapshot stream.

Overlapping intervals were merged before calculating site-year burden.

Manual Recoveries

Manual fixed intervals were added only when a disruption was clearly documented in public source material but was missed, undercaptured, or not parsable by the automated workflow. These additions were logged and retained as auditable fixed intervals in the dataset.

Update Schedule

The dataset can be refreshed when new AHS archive snapshots or notices become available. Cached reruns support parser or manual-adjudication updates without re-scraping. Full fresh scrapes are required when newly posted public records must be incorporated.

Data will be updated quarterly under normal circumstances. The data-through date for each release is listed in the version history on the SORCTracks page.

Known Limitations

SORCTracks is a public-record reconstruction rather than an operational gold standard. Public wording is inconsistent across notices, site names are not always standardized, and some real disruptions cannot be timed defensibly from available source material.

Open-ended disruptions may accumulate hours as the observed source window expands. A disruption with no posted end date will accrue additional hours in each successive data release until a resolution is captured or the disruption is manually closed. This means site-year totals for the most recent period in any given release should be treated as provisional, and cross-version comparisons should account for the data-through date of each release.

Some disruptions may not have been publicly posted at all, or may have been posted but not archived in a form captured by the reconstruction process. SORCTracks therefore represents a lower bound on total disruption burden rather than a complete inventory.

Analytic data files for the Emergency Department layer are embargoed pending manuscript release, at which point they will become publicly accessible through the OSF project. Documentation and audit files are publicly accessible now.
